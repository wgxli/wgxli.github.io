A Geometric Solution of Putnam 2003 B5

Published September 7, 2020｜2 minute read

I've found an elegant geometry-only proof of Putnam 2003 B5, which, to the best of my knowledge, hasn't yet been discovered.

At this point, we have a large equilateral triangle with sidelength 2ℓ2\ell2ℓ, partitioned into twelve smaller triangles:[1]

  1. three triangles with sidelengths a,b,ca, b, ca,b,c,
  2. two triangles with sidelengths a,b,ℓa, b, \ella,b,ℓ,
  3. two triangles with sidelengths a,c,ℓa, c, \ella,c,ℓ,
  4. two triangles with sidelengths b,c,ℓb, c, \ellb,c,ℓ,
  5. and three equilateral triangles, with sidelengths a,b,ca, b, ca,b,c respectively.

The triangles of type 2, 3, and 4 fit together neatly to form two equilateral triangles of sidelength ℓ\ellℓ. We therefore have the following decomposition:

In particular, the area of the triangle we’re looking for is entirely determined by the combined area of the three equilateral triangles with sidelengths aaa, bbb, and ccc. This combined area is proportional to a2+b2+c2a^2 + b^2 + c^2a2+b2+c2. Treating all points as vectors in R2\mathbb{R}^2R2, we obtain

a2+b2+c2=(A−P)2+(B−P)2+(C−P)2=A2+B2+C2+3P2−2 (A+B+C)⋅P=(A2+B2+C2−3O2)+3 (P−O)2,\begin{aligned} &a^2 + b^2 + c^2 \\ &= (A-P)^2 + (B-P)^2 + (C - P)^2 \\ &= A^2 + B^2 + C^2 + 3P^2 - 2 \, (A + B + C) \cdot P \\ &= (A^2 + B^2 + C^2 - 3 O^2) + 3 \, (P - O)^2, \end{aligned}​a2+b2+c2=(A−P)2+(B−P)2+(C−P)2=A2+B2+C2+3P2−2(A+B+C)⋅P=(A2+B2+C2−3O2)+3(P−O)2,​which is entirely determined by the length OPOPOP, as desired.
